PROBLEM & APPROACH

🔥 The Problem
Healthcare billing is broken by design. Payers write rules that change quarterly, denial reasons are buried in 835 ERA files nobody wants to parse, and ops teams spend most of their day on work a machine should handle. At SPRY, the billing team was manually reviewing every denial — a 14-day process that kept AR queues permanently full and cash flow permanently delayed.

The core issue wasn't effort — the team was working hard. It was that no system existed to catch errors before submission, and when denials came back, there was no automation to triage them.
⚡ The Approach
I led the build of an end-to-end AI pipeline: ingest ERA batches → extract ICD-10/CPT codes via NLP → match against payer-specific adjudication rules → run a denial prediction model on flagged claims → auto-resolve high-confidence cases → escalate the rest with context pre-filled for human review.

The key design decision was the confidence threshold. We didn't automate everything — we automated what the model was right about 94%+ of the time, and built a clean escalation UI for the rest. Ops teams trusted it because it was honest about what it didn't know.

Reflection

WHAT I LEARNED

01
The model isn't the product
We had a working denial classifier in week 4. The actual product took 12 more weeks — building the ops UI, the confidence threshold logic, the escalation workflow, and the feedback loop that let the model improve. The ML was 20% of the work.
02
Trust is built in small doses
We went live on 200 claims/week before touching the full queue. Ops teams needed to see it be right consistently before they'd trust it with volume. That patience paid off — adoption was 100% once they trusted the confidence scores.
03
Instrument everything from day one
The dashboard tracking precision, recall, escalation rate, and appeal success rate was live before the pipeline was. Without that, we'd have had no way to tune the confidence threshold or prove the business case for expansion.
